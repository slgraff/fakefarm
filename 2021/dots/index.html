heavenly childhood

The soul carries a list of common questions. Not everyone addresses them with the same rigor, but they exist in some form or another. The questions are the basis of religions and secular movements alike. Of all the big questions, the one that has held the most interest is 'What happens when we die?'

Initially, that question was answered by Christianity laid out in two options: Heaven or Hell. If you are familiar with the sinners prayer, or the doctrines of Christianity they you will most likely agree that it is not much of a choice. Eternal paradise or eternal flames is a seemingly obvious option. It was to me at 6 years old. I recited the sinners prayer and believed my eternal fate was permanently secure. Through my mid-20's I continued to pray that same prayer in some form of fashion just to make sure Jesus heard me. Hell is a scary place.

The Meyers-Briggs personality test calls me an INTJ. Those letters can be summarized to mean that I am curious, experimental, and a big picture kind of guy. As a Christian I used to let my imagination roam the potential of what Paul could be referring to in passages like:

But, as it is written, ‚ÄúWhat no eye has seen, nor ear heard, nor the heart of man imagined, what God has prepared for those who love him‚Äù‚Äî

‚Äî 1 Corinthians 2:9

The little glimpses of eternity through the lens of Christianity was appealing to my emotions the way Christmas Eve was growing up. I knew there was something great tomorrow, so I could rest on those emotions of excitement and undeserved gifts in an unknown place.

Those thoughts were a regular part of my personality before I got married. Partly because of my upbringing being immersed in church culture, and partly because of the little direction from my own father about secular subjects that my imagination and interests were adolescent longer than they should have been.

marriage hell

My attention shifted when I got married. My marriage never got past the OK phase. In fact it was bad for the majority of it. I was raised with the perspective that divorce was a sin, and as consequence I endured many days which otherwise should have never been. It may have been all my pent up positive thinking about how good life is supposed to be that kept me motivated to do something with my life. Because I didn't accept my ex-wife's laziness, unfaithfulness, or depression to stop me from trying to make something of my life.

The most pressing issue for the majority of my marriage was solving our financial situation. The combination of my lack of employable skills and her lack of control on spending got us into debt. I resolved to go back to school to learn a skill.

My first college was a religious one which was nothing more than 4 years of Summer Camp. The only education I received during the time was due to my own side projects and initiatives. But those activities were unguided and ultimately not profitable enough to support my ex-wife's unhealthy spending habits.

I believe in the exercise of listing priorities. That includes relationships. I prioritized my wife above all other friends and family. I was devoted to her with a resolve that was willing to give her all my attention and energy, and only after she was satisfied would I consider time and effort for others. I didn't realize how evil she was that she consumed all my time to the point that all other relationships of friends and family eventually dried up due to lack of investment over the years. During those times I fell on my sword when people would mock me about being late to group events. All this posture lasted 8 years.

For 8 years I worked to get ahead of her brokenness. I didn't realize it was an impossible task. For a period of 3 years I was attending Grad School, running my own business, working a full time job, and applying to Harvard Business School. All of which were activities that were making up for lost time.

Then slowly those things all fell apart. My business, my plans, and my career all came to an end within a 7 month period. Times were not going great.
